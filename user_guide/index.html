Envelope Budgeting

What is envelope budgeting?

The "envelope system" of budgeting is based on the idea that every unit of wealth (every dollar, pound, Euro...) has a job. When money is earned, every unit of that money is immediately assigned a task. For example, suppose I earn a $500 paycheck. I might assign $20 to Entertainment, $100 to Food, $80 to Electricity, $200 to rent, and $100 to retirement savings. When I want to spend some money, I will have a range of "envelopes", each with a certain balance - a certain amount of money available for me to spend on that category. If I have $100 in the Food envelope, I am only allowed to spend $100 on Food - until, that is, the Food envelope is again topped up from the next lot of income. That's the basic idea.

The beauty of envelope budgeting is that if you know how much you spend, on average, on a given category of spending, you can plan ahead by allocating the right amount to that category in advance, so that the money is there when you need it. Say you spend $1,000 a year on electricity. Divide that $1,000 into 52 weeks, and you would allocate $19.23 each week into your electricity envelope. Because you are only allowed to spend the money in this envelope on electricity, the money simply builds up in this envelope, until your next electricity bill arrives - when it is most needed.

Framing a budget

A budget is a plan for how money should be earned and spent. In envelope budgeting terms, this means creating a recurring allocation to each envelope on the basis of how much, on average, is expected to be spent from each envelope over time. For example, if I estimate that I will spend $1,000 per year on electricity, $8,000 a year on food and $2,000 a year on holidays, I could construct a weekly budget in which I allocate the appropriate amount each week, as set out in the following table.
